Grand Plan Announced To Accommodate Population Growth By Building Second Sydney And Melbourne Above First Sydney And Melbourne

4 June 2026 · 2 min

CANBERRA — The federal government has announced an ambitious long-term infrastructure plan to accommodate Australia’s growing population by building additional elevated street-level platforms above Sydney and Melbourne, creating a second CBD and surrounding suburbs directly on top of the existing ones by 2075.

The proposal, described as a “vertical urban continuity framework”, would see major roads, train stations, apartment towers, shopping centres, childcare centres, cafes, bike lanes, vape shops and unresolved social tensions duplicated approximately 300 metres above current street level.

Officials say the plan will allow Australia to continue increasing population without the need for politically difficult conversations about housing, infrastructure, congestion, water, energy, hospitals, schools, wages, social cohesion, or whether endless growth is a substitute for national strategy.

“This is a visionary solution,” said Minister for Cities, Skills and Managed Density Nicole Frampton. “For too long, Australians have thought of space horizontally. However, commutes are already long for many and there are only so many times a government can tell people to move further west before they begin asking whether national planning is just a pyramid scheme with trains. By building upward, we can add millions of residents without requiring them to live in a different postcode, merely a different altitude.”

Under the plan, Sydney’s and Melbourne’s second CBDs would be constructed above the existing CBDs.

Suburbs such as Parramatta, Chatswood, Box Hill, Werribee, Penrith and Dandenong would receive elevated duplicate precincts featuring high-density apartments, retail corridors, transit hubs and carefully designed “community belonging zones” where residents can gather briefly before returning to work to afford rent in either layer.

Planning documents suggest the lower city would remain accessible to legacy residents, freight vehicles, service workers, emergency crews, flooding, heat islands and anyone priced out of the upper platform, while the new elevated level would be marketed as a “premium arrival environment” for skilled migrants, global talent, international students, investors, consultants and people who describe living in a pod as “vibrant”.

Meanwhile, business groups praised the project as essential to maintaining economic growth, warning that without additional vertical capacity Australia may be forced to pursue dangerous alternatives such as productivity reform, wage growth, decentralisation, lower migration, or allowing people to form households before the age of 47.

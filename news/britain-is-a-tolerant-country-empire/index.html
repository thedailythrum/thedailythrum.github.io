“Britain Is A Tolerant Country,” Says Councillor Who Forgot Intolerance Created The Empire

21 June 2026 · 2 min

LONDON — A local councillor has praised Britain as a “deeply tolerant country” during a community cohesion event, apparently forgetting that the British Empire was not assembled through mutual respect, patient dialogue, and a firm commitment to celebrating difference.

The remarks were made at a council forum on inclusion, belonging, and shared values, where attendees were reminded that Britain’s greatest strength has always been its tolerance, apart from the several centuries during which its greatest strength was gunboats, conquest, railways, hospitals, common law, naval supremacy, abolishing suttee, suppressing piracy, redrawing maps, and arriving in foreign ports with the relaxed confidence of a people who had already decided they were in charge.

“Britain has always welcomed diversity,” the councillor said, standing beneath a council banner reading Together Through Understanding. “We are a nation built on kindness, respect, and the belief that everyone deserves to be heard.”

Historians later clarified that Britain was also quite substantially built on refusing to hear people, especially when those people objected to being governed, taxed, converted, surveyed, reorganised, protected, improved, categorised or informed that the Crown would now be managing their affairs for everyone’s benefit.

Council organisers rejected claims that the speech ignored history, insisting the event was focused on “the Britain we choose to remember”.

“We are not here to relitigate the past,” said one facilitator. “We are here to celebrate shared values, which means selecting the bits of history that support our funding application and softly moving past the rest.”

Attendees were then invited to participate in a workshop titled Empire, Belonging, And The Importance Of Listening, during which participants were asked to write down one word that described modern Britain, provided that word was not “conquest”, “opium”, “Rhodesia”, “gunboat”, “railroads”, “partition”, “ending inhuman customs” or “how did we get Hong Kong back?”

“That is progress,” he said. “In the old days, we imposed our values abroad. Now we impose them domestically through equality training, public consultations, speech policies, and community cohesion strategies. It is a completely different moral framework.”

At press time, the council was reportedly preparing a follow-up event explaining that Britain’s legacy is one of peace, provided nobody brings up how the peace was usually achieved and maintained.

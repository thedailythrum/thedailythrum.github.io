Amid Relentless Race-Swapping Of Classical Works, Tarzan Reboot Oddly Keeps Him White

19 July 2026 · 2 min

LOS ANGELES — Producers behind a forthcoming Tarzan reboot have confirmed that the title character will, in a baffling and highly uncharacteristic commitment to source material, remain white.

The decision has surprised industry observers after years in which classical, mythological and historical figures were routinely recast to reflect modern audiences, broader representation and the urgent creative necessity of generating discourse before release.

“Tarzan is a globally recognised character and stories belong to everyone,” said studio diversity consultant Marissa Klent.

“However, after extensive discussion, we concluded that replacing the white man raised by apes in colonial Africa, who swings through trees and communicates through animal cries, with a black actor might generate a form of discourse not currently covered by our inclusion strategy.”

Industry commentators said the reboot represented a rare case in which fidelity to the source material had survived contact with a modern writers’ room.

“For years, audiences have been told that race is incidental, casting should be imaginative and only reactionaries care about historical or cultural consistency,” said film critic Martin Reeves.

“Then somebody suggested a black Tarzan and suddenly everyone discovered subtext.”

The studio said the film would still feature bold contemporary changes, including a female leader of the ape colony, an environmentally conscious antagonist, a non-binary expedition cartographer and a scene in which Jane explains colonialism to several people who are currently being eaten by a leopard.

Tarzan himself, however, will remain white and male.

“This is purely about preserving the essence of the character,” Klent said.

When asked why Helen of Troy in Christopher Nolan’s The Odyssey, Anne Boleyn and Queen Cleopatra had been played by black actresses, and why the Bridgerton streaming adaptation had reimagined Regency England as a racially integrated society, Klent said those examples demonstrated the industry’s courageous willingness to separate European identity from European history.

“European characters are universal,” she said.

“They can be reinterpreted, redistributed and updated to reflect the world as it should have been. Except in certain delicate cases.”

The studio said the decision represented not cowardice but sensitivity.

“We are listening to communities,” Klent said.

“Some communities wish to see themselves represented in classical European stories. Other communities would prefer not to see themselves represented as a half-naked jungle man who beats his chest and talks to gorillas.

Progressive Presenter Releases Book On Pretending Not To Understand Things In Order To Humiliate Callers

13 July 2026 · 4 min

LONDON — A progressive-liberal radio presenter renowned for spending twelve minutes pretending not to understand a caller’s perfectly clear point has released a new book teaching aspiring broadcasters how to convert political disagreement into a procedural competency test.

“But In Your Previous Breath You Said Something Else, So It’s Not That, Then, Is It?”, subtitled A Practical Guide to Mogging Right-Wingers offers readers a practical guide to interrupting callers, demanding increasingly specific definitions, ignoring the obvious substance of what they are saying and then declaring victory when they become flustered.

The book includes full chapters on asking callers to define ordinary words, demanding citations for lived experience and treating any emotional reaction to prolonged bad-faith questioning as proof that the caller’s original argument lacked intellectual rigour.

“I’ve got nothing against my callers,” the presenter explained.

“I’m just saying that it’s not my fault if I’m a seasoned orator with 25 years of experience on live radio and they’re a roofer from Dagenham who thought they were ringing in to make one point about so-called ‘mass migration’.”

The presenter said this imbalance did not make the exchange unfair, but merely demonstrated the importance of preparation.

“If he didn’t want to be publicly dismantled, he should have spent the previous hours researching graphs, statistics and interpreting government policies that support his position before he calls a national radio station to make a point that falls apart at the first mildly persistent question.”

He added that callers who wished to discuss immigration should arrive with a working knowledge of net migration figures, asylum law, labour-market participation, housing supply, public spending, demographic projections, Treasury methodology and the precise wording of every ministerial statement issued since 1997.

“They’re perfectly entitled to have an opinion,” he said.

“But if they accuse me of interrupting them, redefine the terms, move the burden of proof and cut to adverts whenever they begin landing a point, maybe they should ask themselves where they got their opinions from in the first place.”

He said many callers claimed to rely on things they had seen, experienced or watched happen around them, rather than waiting for the correct interpretation to be issued by a broadcaster.

“Only they don’t,” he said. “Not really.”

“Their views are usually just an average of whatever they’ve heard from people like Nigel Farage or Tommy Robinson, or from media outlets such as the Murdoch press or The Lotus Eaters, rather than the considered interpretation of a cooler head like mine.”

The presenter said this distinction was important because opinions formed through direct observation could become dangerously detached from the approved analytical framework.

“People think that because they live somewhere, raise children there, work there, pay rent there and notice that it has changed, they possess some special insight into what is happening,” he said.

“But lived experience is no substitute for a man in a studio explaining why they have misunderstood it.”

A chapter, titled Where Did You Hear That?, advises presenters to treat the provenance of an opinion as more important than whether it is true.

“Once you establish that a caller has listened to the wrong person, you no longer need to address the substance,” the book explains.

“The argument has been successfully converted into a hygiene issue.”

A chapter titled Have You Got A Source For That? teaches presenters how to demand documentary proof for observations that would ordinarily be obvious to anyone who had visited a large town within the past decade.

If the caller produces a statistic, readers are advised to dispute the source.

If the source is official, question the methodology.

If the methodology is sound, insist that correlation is not causation.

If the caller survives all three stages, ask whether he has considered that his tone may be part of the problem.

Another chapter, I’m Just Asking Questions, explains how to ask the same question six different ways until the caller produces a slightly different answer, which can then be presented as a humiliating contradiction.

“The trick is to remain calm,” the presenter said.

“Calmness signals truth. Anger signals that the other person is wrong.”

The book also recommends referring to unpopular government policy as “complex”, public frustration as “anecdotal”, and any pattern visible across several towns as “something we should be cautious about generalising from”.

At publishing time, the presenter was denying that his tactics amounted to bullying, explaining that bullying required an imbalance of power while using twenty-five years of live-broadcast experience, control of the microphone and a producer hovering over the disconnect button to interrogate ordinary callers who were not professionally immersed in politics and often lacked the vocabulary to explain what they could plainly see.

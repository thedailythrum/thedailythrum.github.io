Unvaccinated Cops Welcomed Back Years After Trauma Of The Century Memory-Holed

18 June 2026 · 4 min

SYDNEY — Police officers who refused the COVID vaccine have been warmly invited to return to the force years after being stood down, dismissed, shamed, isolated, treated as operational contaminants and informed that public service was impossible without total medical compliance.

The move comes after officials determined that enough time had passed for the entire episode to be discussed in the emotionally neutral language of “workforce recovery”, “re-engagement”, “lessons learned” and “moving forward”, rather than the more difficult language of “we bullied people during a panic and now need staff”.

“We are pleased to welcome former officers back into the organisation,” said Police Workforce Renewal Director Carla Pickering. “Our focus is on rebuilding capacity, honouring service and ensuring everyone feels valued as part of the policing family.”

Asked whether the returning officers had felt valued when they were removed from the policing family for declining a medical intervention, Pickering said it was important not to litigate the past, particularly parts of the past that had aged poorly.

“At the time, decisions were made based on the best available advice, operational requirements, public health settings, community expectations and the moral atmosphere of 2021, which was completely insane but very professionally documented,” she said.

Former constable Jordan Prebble, who lost his job after refusing the vaccine and now works as a private security contractor, described the invitation as “too little, too late”.

“They bullied us,” Prebble said. “I wanted to make a difference in the community and they called me uncooperative. Now I make three times the amount I did with a lower risk to life, fewer domestic callouts, less paperwork, and nobody from HR emailing me about ‘shared values’.”

Prebble said the hardest part was not losing the job, but watching colleagues and senior officers pretend the moral certainty of the period had never happened.

“It wasn’t just policy,” he said. “It was contempt. They acted like we were dirty, selfish, stupid, dangerous and unfit to serve. Now they’re short-staffed and suddenly I’m a valued former officer with transferable skills.”

Police officials denied that the recruitment effort represented a reversal, apology or admission that previous policies had been excessive.

“This is not about reopening old wounds,” Pickering said. “This is about acknowledging that some former officers may wish to return to an organisation that removed them from service, damaged their careers and then waited several years before sending a friendly email beginning ‘Dear valued former colleague’.”

The email reportedly encouraged former officers to “consider coming home”, a phrase several recipients described as “bold” given that home had changed the locks during a period when every institution in the country was competing to demonstrate its virtue by finding new categories of citizen to exclude.

Union representatives welcomed the recruitment initiative but said more needed to be done to rebuild trust.

“You can’t just pretend this was a minor administrative disagreement,” said former police association delegate Nathan Norton. “People lost careers, friendships, income, reputation and years of their lives. Then the whole country collectively decided the era was too awkward to remember and moved on to housing, energy prices and whatever the next emergency was.”

Norton said institutions had become very skilled at exiting moral panics without accountability.

“There’s never a reckoning,” he said. “There’s just a new phrase. Yesterday’s dangerous dissident becomes today’s experienced candidate once the crisis that required it has quietly ended, been buried by other news cycles and the spreadsheet says staffing is down.”

A senior police source said many managers who enforced the mandate now privately concede the period was “messy”.

“It was ‘messy’ when my girlfriend left me and took the dog,” the source said. “It’s not messy when the government suspends your livelihood, excludes you from society, tells everyone you’re a threat, then quietly sends a recruitment email four years later because the roster’s understaffed.”

Government ministers praised the police force for its inclusive recruitment approach.

“This demonstrates our commitment to healing,” said Public Sector Minister Marissa Klent. “We cannot change the past, but we can invite those affected by past decisions to return under the leadership structures that made those decisions and have since experienced no consequences.”

Klent said there were no plans for a formal apology, though the government was considering a “reflective acknowledgement of complex workforce impacts” if it could be worded in a way that admitted nothing.

Prebble said he had no intention of returning.

“I miss the uniform sometimes,” he said. “I miss the good cops. I miss the idea of the job. But I don’t miss being lectured about duty by people who folded instantly when the politics changed.”

At the time of publication, police officials had reassured former officers that the organisation had changed, grown, learned valuable lessons and remained deeply committed to the same leadership culture that produced the original decision.

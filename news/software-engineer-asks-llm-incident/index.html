Software Engineer Asks LLM How To Tell Ops Team “It’s Friday, It’s 4:45, Shove Your Incident”

1 July 2026 · 5 min

SYDNEY — Senior software engineer Ryan Hollis reportedly consulted a large language model late Friday afternoon after joining a Teams call in which the operations team appeared to be seeking confirmation that a production incident was serious enough to make everyone else’s weekend worse.

The incident began at 4:43pm when L1 support reported that several customers were unable to upload supporting documents through an insurance claims portal built in 2018, causing L1 support to fear the issue might become “visible” if claimants continued expecting the website to work.

Hollis, who had emotionally left the company at 3:12pm, joined the call with his camera off and the dead-eyed courtesy of a man whose employment arrangement had turned sour several years earlier.

According to sources, L1 support lead Nathan Norton opened the call by explaining that he had seen a number of errors in the logs and wanted engineering to “take a look” before the weekend.

“What kind of errors?” Hollis asked.

“Errors when people attempt to submit claims,” Norton said.

“Right,” Hollis replied, staring at the wall.

Several witnesses confirmed that Hollis then opened an AI assistant in another browser window and typed the following prompt into the LLM:

How do I professionally tell the ops team: “It’s Friday, it’s 4:45, shove your incident, no one is going to die from this”?

The model suggested a more measured response.

Thanks for flagging this. Given the timing, current visibility, and incomplete impact assessment, I suggest we capture the relevant evidence, confirm the affected paths, and determine whether immediate engineering intervention is warranted.

Hollis reportedly leaned back in his chair.

“That’s good,” he said. “That sounds like I’m participating.”

Norton continued explaining that several submissions had failed, the issue could become more visible over the weekend, and leadership would likely be unhappy if the situation escalated.

Hollis listened carefully, nodded at a screen nobody could see, and typed another prompt.

Make it sound like I understand the risk but am not volunteering to spend Friday evening spelunking through an insurance claims portal assembled during the Turnbull era.

The LLM obliged.

I understand the potential customer and business impact. At this stage, we should establish severity by confirming volume, recurrence, and whether the failures are increasing. Without that information, immediate remediation may be premature.

“That should buy me my Friday back,” Hollis said, pleased with himself.

Sources familiar with the call said the operations team appeared less interested in Hollis fixing the issue than in ensuring a senior engineer had been present when the decision was made not to fix it.

“They didn’t need Ryan to solve it,” said one developer. “They needed Ryan to be adjacent to it, so if anyone asked later, they could say the person from the engineering team who owns the service was engaged.”

Hollis later told The Daily Thrum this was the precise moment he realised he was not on a troubleshooting call, but inside an accountability distribution mechanism.

“The service is old. The logs, wherever exist, are noisy. The impact is unclear. Some claims aren’t going through, which obviously isn’t ideal,” Hollis said.

“But here’s my position: if this turns into a headline, I’d prefer my name to appear nowhere near the words ‘last engineer consulted before weekend outage’.”

As Norton continued outlining possible customer impact, Hollis asked the LLM for “a phrase that sounds supportive but creates no obligations.”

“I’m giving this company 50 hours of my week, plus whatever remains of my posture, eyesight and desire to speak to other human beings. I don’t see why I should also donate my Friday evening to a service nobody funded properly, documented properly, or replaced when the first warning signs appeared in 2019.”

The model responded:

I’m happy to provide input on severity, but I would suggest support continue gathering evidence before we determine whether this requires immediate engineering action.

Hollis spoke the sentence into his headset.

“If Norton got the hint, ending the call would look like it was his decision”, Hollis later confessed. “If this turned into a business-impacting crater, I wouldn’t be the bloke who said we could park it.”

He paused.

“Which, to be clear, was exactly what I was trying to make happen.”

When Norton asked whether Hollis could stay available for another thirty minutes while they gathered more data, Hollis returned to the LLM.

I’m running late for my weekend doomscrolling binge. Make this sound like no, but in a way that won’t get back to HR.

The model suggested:

I have a hard stop shortly, but please document any additional findings in the incident channel. If the impact materially changes, it can be escalated through the normal after-hours process.

Hollis spoke the response.

The call ended at 5:06pm with no clear resolution, three action items, and broad agreement that the issue should continue to be monitored by people who were not Ryan Hollis.

The engineer later clarified that it’s important not to teach people that he’d be forever available on demand for late-Friday incidents, noting that once that expectation is set, it becomes the default rather than the exception.

At publishing time, Hollis could be heard muttering, “Where the fuck were LLMs in 2021?” as he remembered earlier incidents where he had been forced to sit silently on Teams saying “yeah”, “right”, and “let me check” until support slowly reached the same conclusion without him.

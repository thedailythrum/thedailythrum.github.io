James O’Brien Defeats “We Have The Recipes” Argument By Asking Caller To Recite Chicken Biryani From Memory

21 July 2026 · 3 min

LONDON — Broadcaster James O’Brien has decisively disproved the claim that Britain can retain foreign cuisine without permanently importing the populations associated with it, after a caller failed to recite a complete chicken biryani recipe live on air.

The exchange began when caller Dave from Lancashire argued that multiculturalism was no longer required to secure access to international food because “we have the recipes”.

O’Brien did not skip a beat.

“What’s the recipe for chicken biryani?” he demanded, applying the traditional evidentiary standard under which a civilisation does not possess knowledge unless a randomly selected man can reproduce it from memory while being interrupted by a professional broadcaster.

The caller hesitated.

“What?” he finally said.

“You heard me, mate. You said you have the recipes. So. Chicken biryani. Go.”

Dave began to explain that “we” referred to the country collectively, including cookbooks, restaurants, websites, archived television programmes and several million people capable of finding the answer in under ten seconds.

O’Brien cut him off.

“Yes, yes, I understand all that. So, by what you’ve gone with, if you were to make a chicken biryani, you must have ‘the recipe’. So how do I make a chicken biryani?”

“I don’t know. There must be a recipe in a cookbook or something.”

“So you don’t have the recipe.”

“No, I said we have the recipes.”

“And you are part of we. So I’m asking you for one.”

The caller attempted to explain that national possession did not require each citizen to memorise every item collectively available to the country.

O’Brien rejected the distinction.

“Then who has the recipe?” he asked.

“The internet.”

“And who put it on the internet? Was it Steve from Lancashire?”

“I don’t know. Someone who knows how to make chicken biryani?”

“And who is statistically more likely to know how to make a chicken biryani? A Steve or a Mohammed?”

The caller paused.

“Probably Mohammed.”

O’Brien leaned back, satisfied.

“So you do need Mohammed.”

“No, I need the recipe.”

“And who has the recipe?”

“…Mohammed, maybe?”

“Exactly,” O’Brien declared triumphantly. “So, Dave, let’s see what you’ve done so far. You began by saying Britain has the recipes. We then discovered that you personally do not have the recipe, you would need to look it up, somebody else had to put it there, and that person was statistically more likely to come from the region biryani came from than from Lancashire.”

“Erm… yeah, but… but t— the—”

“Before you go, mate, will you admit that you were catastrophically, utterly and comprehensively wrong? Or will you continue believing that people with a darker skin complexion than yours do not need to be here because ‘we have the recipes’?”

“I said the recipe is on the internet.”

O’Brien thanked him for proving the point and cut the line.

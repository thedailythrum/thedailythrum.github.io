“Deployment Needs A Manual Step In This Repo”: Starmer’s Handover To Burnham Now Underway

18 July 2026 · 3 min

LONDON — Sir Keir Starmer’s handover to Andy Burnham is reportedly underway after the outgoing Prime Minister began transferring critical government knowledge in the form of vague comments, half-updated documents and warnings that several important national systems “need manual steps”.

According to Downing Street sources, Burnham was invited to a leadership transition session on Tuesday morning titled Handover — Final FINAL, during which Starmer walked him through the current operating model of the country using a 47-page document last updated during the May cabinet.

“Most of it is self-explanatory,” Starmer reportedly said, opening a folder called PM Stuff. “The main thing is not to touch the economy pipeline after Thursday, because it triggers Scotland.”

Sources said Burnham initially appeared confident, but became concerned after discovering several major policy areas were maintained through undocumented conventions, legacy assumptions, ministerial workarounds, civil service folklore and one spreadsheet called Master Tracker v9 - USE THIS ONE ACTUALLY.

“Deployment needs a manual step in this repo,” Starmer explained, pointing at a diagram of British governance that appeared to have been drawn during a rail strike. “If you skip it, the whole thing looks like it worked, but then Northern Ireland breaks three days later.”

Burnham was then shown a list of recurring issues marked Known Problems — Do Not Fix Before Spending Review, including housing, migration, energy bills, NHS waiting lists, rail capacity, small boats, public trust and the growing sense among voters that nobody knows who is in charge of anything.

“Some of these look serious,” Burnham said.

“Yes,” Starmer replied. “That’s why we moved them to a separate board.”

Officials said the handover became tense when Burnham asked whether there was a single source of truth for the government’s commitments.

“There are several,” Starmer said. “There’s the manifesto, the Treasury view, the Number 10 view, the Cabinet Office view, what we told the unions, what we told the markets, what we told Washington, and what Rachel says we can still afford if nobody asks for numbers.”

Burnham was also briefed on emergency procedures, including how to respond to a fiscal crisis, a terror alert, a transport shutdown, a major cyber incident, or a journalist asking whether the government had any principles that could survive contact with polling data.

“For most incidents, you’ll want to say the situation is complex, thank emergency services, announce a review, then wait to see whether it becomes someone else’s problem,” Starmer said. “If it trends for more than forty-eight hours, convene stakeholders.”

At press time, Starmer was reminding Burnham that the country could only be released to production by one civil servant called Margaret, who worked Tuesdays to Thursdays and was currently on leave.

We Don't Have “Enough 1776” In Australia—And That's A Good Thing

16 July 2026 · 3 min

By Liam Riestead-Schittlieb (he/him) | Opinions expressed are those of the author and may become mandatory following consultation.

There are “libertarian political commentators” in Australia—whatever that means; it is probably just big words for “wankers”—who use the slogan:

Too much 1984, not enough 1776

It appears on T-shirts, podcasts and X profiles.

There is only one problem.

This is Australia.

You do not have free speech here. Natural rights are not guaranteed. Government by consent is not guaranteed.

In Australia, these things are merely implied and are broadly interpretable.

This is not America, where citizens carry on as though liberty is something they possess inherently rather than a conditional benefit administered by qualified adults. Australia has taken a more sensible approach. Here, freedom is not a rigid entitlement written down in language ordinary people might understand and later use against the government.

Freedom principle inferred by judges from the structure of representative government, then balanced against whichever restriction Parliament has most recently described as necessary, proportionate and supported by community expectations.

This is called maturity. Americans insist that certain rights are inalienable. Australians understand that nothing is inalienable once a minister has received sufficiently alarming advice from a department.

Your right to speak exists until your speech creates harm. Your right to protest exists until your protest becomes disruptive. Your right to privacy exists until accessing your information is important for social cohesion. Your right to refuse exists until refusal creates friction for a government service.

Your right to political participation exists provided you do not use it to undermine confidence in the political system.

You should be grateful.

The phrase “government by consent” is particularly inappropriate here. Australians consent through elections. Every few years, we choose which major party will implement broadly similar policies using slightly different branding. Once the election is over, consent has been obtained and it does not need to be renewed every time the government introduces something nobody remembers voting for.

You cannot expect citizens to approve every surveillance power, speech restriction, digital identity system, emergency measure, migration target, data-sharing agreement or international commitment. Most people are busy. That is why we have representatives. Representatives consent on your behalf, often without understanding the legislation either. This is efficient because it prevents public ignorance from interfering with institutional momentum.

The American revolutionaries had the opposite idea. They believed governments derived their legitimate power from the consent of the governed and that people could alter or abolish governments that became destructive of their rights. This is plainly unsuitable for Australia. Every time a government ignored voters, expanded its own authority, suppressed lawful dissent, transferred power to unaccountable bodies or imposed obligations never presented during an election, some idiot might conclude that consent had been withdrawn.

Where would it end? Accountability?

Besides, nobody orders you to stop speaking anyway. If you think so, you have been conditioned by online grifters who make money telling you that you are one brave podcast subscription away from living under Stalin. Instead, they merely create an environment in which speaking becomes progressively more expensive. Your post is labelled, your reach declines, your employer receives complaints, your bank conducts a review, police visit for a conversation, and eventually you just choose silence voluntarily.

Coercion is unnecessary when consequences are well signposted.

So no, we do not need more 1776. We need less of it.

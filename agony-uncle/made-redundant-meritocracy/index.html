I've Been Made Redundant From A Principal Engineer Role And Now I’m Discovering The Meritocracy Was A Talent Acquisition Funnel

30 June 2026 · 4 min

Dear Agony Uncle,

I was made redundant from a Principal Engineer role and I have been struggling to find another role for four months now.

My savings are dwindling.

This has been difficult to process because, if I am honest, I thought this kind of predicament would happen to other people. People who had not studied hard enough. People who had not worked hard enough. People who had not made the correct choices.

Me? I did the right things. I became senior, then principal, mentored less experienced devs, reduced cloud spend, modernised delivery pipelines, I prevented incidents nobody thanked me for because nothing exploded, which apparently makes the work invisible… and lo and fucking behold, there’s been a perfect storm of hire freezes across the board, fucking chatbots — chatbots! — that can scaffold entire CRUD applications, CI/CD YAML files and IaC scripts, more of my colleagues got thrown out into the market at the same time, ‘cause someone discovered they could pay three people in a different time zone the price of one of you and still hit their quarterly targets, and suddenly “global talent strategy” became a moral virtue instead of a cost-cutting exercise.

I’ve sent 350 applications around and I either got ghosted or got rejected at the second interview. I have tailored applications. I have networked. I have pretended to be excited about companies whose entire mission appears to be moving XML between vendors. And still nothing. What can I do?

Yours,

Accidentally Humbled

Dear Accidentally Humbled,

Yeah, it’s a bit shit, hey.

Unfortunately, noticing that will not help.

So, accept the first great humiliation of job searching: you are not applying as yourself. You are applying as a customised fictional entity whose entire reason for being just happens to align perfectly with the exact mission of whichever company has advertised the role.

This is especially important in your cover letters. You cannot write like a normal person. A normal person says, “I need work, I have relevant experience, this job looks tolerable enough and I hope the people who work there are not complete arseholes”. That is human. And therefore it must be removed.

Instead, you must write as though your deepest passion in life has always been whatever the company currently does. If they process insurance claims, you have long been fascinated by the intersection of customer trust, scalable platforms, and claims transformation. If they sell workforce analytics, you believe deeply in helping organisations make better people decisions through data-driven insight, which is a polite way of saying “spreadsheets about staff anxiety”. If they run a payments platform, you are excited by the opportunity to support resilient financial infrastructure at scale, even though what you really mean is “I can probably fix your retries.” If they are a start-up using AI to improve procurement workflows, you must say that for as long as you can remember, you have always been passionate about optimising procurement lifecycles through intelligent automation, and the transformative potential of AI-assisted decision-making.

Does this sound like you? Or does it sound like a man who has been passed through three ATS filters, a recruiter screening call, and a leadership competency framework? I’d wager it’s the latter.

The second thing you must do is stop assuming that being qualified is enough. No one gives a kanga’s arse about your qualifications in isolation. Qualification is only the raw material. The application process is theatre, and you are expected to know your lines.

Your CV must not merely list what you did. Otherwise it’d just say “I plopped down on my ergonomic chair and typed things into a laptop, things moved on a screen and occasionally some royalty would come and ask me ‘how are we tracking for feature/PROJ-12345?”’

Instead, it must make the hiring manager feel that bringing you in would reduce their personal anxiety. 

Do not say “Led platform modernisation.” Say “Led platform modernisation across legacy services, reducing deployment risk and improving delivery confidence for engineering teams”.

Do not say “Mentored developers.” Say “Provided technical direction and mentoring to engineers, improving system ownership, design quality, and delivery consistency.”

Do not say “My Project Manager kept blocking delivery with continuous status update requests.” Say “Worked cross-functionally with delivery stakeholders to improve communication flow, reduce unnecessary reporting overhead, and keep engineering teams focused on delivering value.”

The third thing you must do is to go and lift heavy shit. No, stay with me. Put some Five Finger on. I like “Under and Over It”. It’s on my gym playlist. You may like “War Is The Answer” or “Boots And Blood”. Whatever. I don’t give a fuck. The important thing is that your psyche is going to change if you go out and lift heavy shit instead of sitting there marinating in your own head about the job search.

No cunt is going to do it for you.

Yours in managed decline,

Agony Uncle
